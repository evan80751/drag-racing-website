What is Drag Racing?

Drag racing is the ultimate test of acceleration and timing. Specifically bracket drag racing, is a competitive form of drag racing that balances the field for cars of all performance levels. Each driver sets a dial in time predicting how fast their car will run. The slower cars gets a "head start" based on the difference in times, and the winner is the driver who comes closest to their prediction without going faster, getting beat by the slower car reaching the line first, and all revolves around timing from off the start line.

It's not just about having the fastest car, it's about reaction time, driving skill, and consistency. It's a balance of the perfect combination of power, and traction, and your ability to manage the car.

Races are often won or lost by thousandths of a second!

History of Drag Racing

Drag racing began in the late 1940's and early 1950's, when hot rod enthusiast moved their street racing to abandoned airstrips for safety. The sport quickly organized, with the National Hot Road Association (NHRA) forming in 1951.

The first official drag strip opened in 1950, and by the 1960's, drag racing had become a professional motorsport with dedicated tracks and standardized rules. Today, the NHRA events draw hundreds of thousands of spectators annually.

Iconic Drag Racing Venues

Julesburg Dragstrip - Julesburg, Colorado

Julesburg Dragstrip holds the distinction of being one of the oldest continuously operating drag strips in the country. Located in rural Colorado, this historic 1/4 mile track has been the proving ground for countless racers since the 1950's.

What makes Julesburg special is its authentic old-school atmosphere and the fact that it's still family-owned and operated. Racing here feels like stepping back in time to drag racings golden era.

The venue is a staple on the NHRA circuit and has hosted countless legendary events with its one-of-a-kind atmosphere.

Lucas Oil Indianapolis Raceway - Indianapolis, Indiana

Indianapolis Raceway Park is home to the prestigious U.S. Nations, the largest and most historic drag racing event in the world. Thousands of fans flock here every year to witness some of the fastest runs and greatest moment in drag racing history.

Gainesville Raceway - Gainesville, Florida

Gainesville Raceway is best known for the NHRA Gatornationals, one of the sport's crown jewel events. Since opening in 1969, it has been the stage for record-setting performances and is a must-visit venue for any drag racing enthusiast.

Pomona Raceway - Pomona, California

Pomona Raceway, officially known as Auto Club Raceway at Pomona, is drag racing's home in Southern California. It hosts both the NHRA season opener (Winternationals) and the season finale, making it a bookend to the professional drag racing calendar.

Types of Drag Racing

1/4 Mile: The traditional distance, 1,320 feet of pure acceleration. Time typically ranges from 4 second (Top Fuel) to 18+ seconds (street cars).

1/8 Mile: 660 feet, popular at smaller tracks and for safety reasons. Quicker events but still intense competition.

Classes: From Top Fuel dragsters hitting 330+ mph to street-legal cars running what you drove to the track.

  • Professional Categories (Top Fuel, Funny Car, Pro Stock, etc.)
  • Sportsman Categories (Super comp, Stock, Super Stock, etc)
  • Street & Grudge Racing

Types: There are multiple different types of drag racing types, and how the race is orchestrated and the rules involved.

  • Bracket Racing - Handicap system using dial-in times; closest to prediction without producing a faster time wins.
  • Index Racing - Each class has a dixed index time (like 9.90 or 10.90); breaking under it is a loss.
  • Heads-Up Racing - First to the finish line wins; no handicaps.
